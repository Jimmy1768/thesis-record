# Open Questions

- What makes an Operator Node distinct from a freelancer with AI tools?
- What coordination costs does AI actually reduce?
- Which transaction costs cannot be reduced by AI?
- What legal form would operator nodes use?
- How do operator nodes gain trust and market access?
- When does a network of nodes become a firm in practice?
- Which industries are most likely to show early evidence?
- Which historical analogues are closest, and where do they fail?
- What data can falsify the theory within 1, 3, or 5 years?
- Can BTOS or another verified source be linked to productivity, revenue,
  employment, or concentration outcomes by firm size, rather than adoption
  alone?
- What dataset can directly observe one-human nonemployer AI use, receipts,
  survival, and transitions into employer firms?
- Which transaction-cost metrics can be measured separately from task-time
  metrics in real procurement, contracting, support, or platform workflows?
- Do platform escrow, arbitration, reputation, insurance, certification,
  audit-log, SLA, or payment systems measurably reduce dispute, monitoring, or
  enforcement costs for one-person suppliers?
- Can BTOS AI supplement data be linked to Census longitudinal business data
  to test whether AI adopters later change employment, payroll, receipts,
  survival, or firm size?
- Are AI-related employment decreases concentrated in firms with broad
  functional integration and operational investment, and does that show
  automation or broader reorganization?
- Do public reputation/evaluation mechanisms help new one-person suppliers, or
  do they create entry barriers when workers lack initial verifiable history?
- Can NES/AIES-NES nonemployer receipt-size and industry data be linked to an
  AI-exposure measure without confusing general digital services growth with
  AI-enabled one-human firms?
- What dataset observes nonemployer survival, churn, and transition into
  employer status while also identifying AI use or AI exposure?
- Can BFS employer-formation measures be used as a conservative test of
  node-becomes-firm transitions, or does the lack of nonemployer-to-employer
  linkage make that proxy too indirect?
- Are there administrative payroll, HRIS, or firmographic datasets that
  directly measure span of control, manager-to-worker ratios, support-staff
  ratios, or org-chart depth after AI adoption?
- Which public exposure proxy should be accepted first, and how should it be
  prevented from being mistaken for nonemployer AI-use evidence?
- What minimum internal or external case fields are required before a node-like
  case can be classified as feasibility evidence?
- Which transaction-cost fields can the company or partners collect without
  exposing client-sensitive information?
- Which datasets can distinguish remote salaried employees from contractors,
  vendors, owner-operators, and AI labor services by role, sector, and output?
- In which AI-exposed roles should remote employment persistence trigger
  revision or rejection of the strong employment-unbundling thesis?
- If the thesis is directionally correct, what measurable categories separate
  creators, niche human providers, and experiencers from existing
  owner/employee/self-employed categories?
- What evidence would show that compensated human preference validation is
  becoming a real labor market rather than unpaid reviews, passive telemetry,
  synthetic-user simulation, or elite taste-making?
- The first governed private source is the company operator network. What
  private storage location and empty intake manifest should be used before any
  data is collected?
- What private storage, retention, redaction, aggregation, and disclosure
  workflow should be implemented before any HRIS, payroll, procurement, node,
  AI-labor-service, or evaluator data is collected?
