## Source Type

## Evidence Category

- Task productivity: no.
- Transaction-cost evidence: yes, for information frictions in online hiring.
- Firm-boundary evidence: no direct firm-boundary outcome.
- Platform-governance evidence: yes, for public evaluations as a market
  information mechanism.

## Core Claims

- Hiring inexperienced workers produces public information about worker
  ability.
- In an online marketplace experiment, hiring workers and providing more
  detailed public evaluations improved later employment outcomes.
- The paper interprets some workers as inefficiently unemployed under plausible
  assumptions, consistent with information frictions and entry barriers.

## Evidence Used

The AEA abstract reports a field experiment in which 952 randomly selected
workers were hired and given detailed or coarse public evaluations. Hiring and
detailed evaluations improved subsequent employment outcomes, and estimated
market-level benefits exceeded costs under plausible assumptions.

## Relevance To Operator Node Hypothesis

This supports the narrow claim that evaluation and reputation infrastructure
can reduce information frictions in online contracting. It also adds a
criticism: without credible initial signals, new one-person suppliers may face
inefficient entry barriers even on digital platforms.

## Objections Or Limitations

- The source studies entry-level workers in an online labor market, not
  AI-enabled Operator Nodes.
- It improves the case for information/evaluation mechanisms, but it does not
  measure escrow, arbitration, audit logs, insurance, procurement, liability,
  or firm-boundary change.
- It does not show that one-person suppliers outperform firms; it shows that
  missing information can suppress market hiring.
