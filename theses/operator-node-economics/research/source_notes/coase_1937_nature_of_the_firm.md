## Core Claims

- Firms exist partly because using the price mechanism is costly.
- A firm substitutes a bounded authority relationship for repeated market
  contracting.
- Longer contracts reduce repeated contracting but remain incomplete because
  future needs are hard to specify.
- Firm boundaries are marginal and comparative: internal organization is used
  until it is no cheaper than market exchange or organization by another
  entrepreneur.
- Communication and managerial technologies can affect firm size in either
  direction depending on whether they reduce internal or market transaction
  costs more.

## Direct Reinspection

Directly reinspected on 2026-06-11 from an archived JSTOR PDF copy of the
article. The PDF is page-image based, so inspection was visual rather than text
extracted.

Pages/sections inspected:

- p. 388: Coase contrasts price-coordinated market transactions with
  entrepreneur-coordinated transactions inside the firm.
- pp. 390-391: Coase identifies costs of using the price mechanism, including
  discovering relevant prices and negotiating/concluding separate contracts;
  he also defines the employment contract as bounded obedience to an
  entrepreneur.
- p. 393: Coase discusses government/regulatory treatment and defines the firm
  as a system of relationships dependent on entrepreneurial direction.
- p. 394: Coase states the marginal boundary condition where internal
  organizing costs equal open-market or other-entrepreneur organizing costs.
- pp. 397-398: Coase discusses spatial distribution, telephone/telegraph
  effects, managerial technique, and integration.

Safe for future prose:

- Firms and markets are alternative methods of coordinating production.
- Coase's firm-boundary argument is comparative and marginal.
- Market contracting costs include discovering prices and negotiating or
  concluding separate contracts.
- A firm may grow when organizing another transaction internally is cheaper
  than market exchange or organization by another entrepreneur.
- Technology can increase firm size when it lowers internal organizing costs;
  whether a technology shrinks firms depends on relative effects on internal
  versus market-use costs.

Claims still needing caution:

- Coase does not say lower communication or coordination costs necessarily
  reduce firm size.
- Coase does not support any direct claim about AI or Operator Nodes.
- The Operator Node implication is an interpretation: AI must be tested as a
  relative-cost shift, not assumed from Coase.

Interpretations weakened or corrected:

- The earlier broad phrasing "what happens when transaction costs collapse" is
  too loose. Coase supports a relative-cost comparison, not a general collapse
  story.
- Coase's technology discussion explicitly allows firm-size increases.

## Relevance To Operator Node Hypothesis

Coase supports the correct framing: the Operator Node hypothesis must be tested
as a relative-cost claim. AI-enabled nodes matter only if they reduce
market/protocol transaction costs relative to internal corporate organization
costs for specific transaction classes.

## Objections Or Limitations

- Coase's argument is comparative, not anti-firm.
- If AI reduces internal managerial costs as much as or more than market
  transaction costs, the Coasean prediction could be larger or more efficient
  firms.
- A one-human Operator Node may itself be a firm if it organizes transactions
  through entrepreneurial direction.
