## Bibliographic Reference

Rachel Arledge, "How AI and Other Technology Impacted Businesses and Workers,"
U.S. Census Bureau America Counts, September 17, 2025.

## Source Type

Official government statistical summary based on the 2023 Annual Business
Survey, which produced 2022 data.

## Evidence Category

- Task productivity: no.
- Transaction-cost evidence: no.
- Firm-boundary evidence: partial employer-business workforce impact evidence;
  no nonemployer, outsourcing, management-layer, or node-to-firm transition
  evidence.
- Adoption-by-size evidence: not the main evidence used here.

## Core Claims

- Employer businesses most often reported that adopting tracked technologies,
  including AI, did not change worker numbers overall between 2020 and 2022.
- When worker-number changes occurred, most technologies except robotics were
  more likely to increase than decrease worker numbers.
- Most businesses also reported little or no impact on worker skill levels.
- AI was the tracked technology most often cited as positively affecting worker
  skill level.

## Evidence Used

The article reports on the 2023 Annual Business Survey, covering employer
businesses. It states that businesses most often reported no overall worker
number change after adopting AI, specialized software, robotics, cloud-based
technology, or specialized equipment. It also reports that most businesses
found little or no worker-skill impact, while AI had the largest positive
worker-skill response among tracked technologies.

## Relevance To Operator Node Hypothesis

This weakens premature claims that AI adoption is already shrinking firms or
collapsing employment boundaries. It is useful disconfirming evidence for
P001/P005-style claims unless later evidence shows delayed boundary effects.

## Objections Or Limitations

- The article summarizes 2022 data collected in the 2023 ABS, so it predates
  the 2025-2026 generative-AI diffusion measured by BTOS.
- It covers employer businesses, not nonemployer Operator Nodes.
- It measures worker-number and skill impacts, not transaction costs,
  outsourcing, management layers, firm formation, or market concentration.
- It is an official summary rather than a causal study.
