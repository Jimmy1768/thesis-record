## Core Claims

- A randomized controlled field experiment on eBay compared sales under a
  high-reputation established identity with matched sales under new seller
  identities controlled by the same seller.
- The established identity received higher willingness to pay.
- A subsidiary experiment found that one or two negative feedbacks for new
  sellers did not affect willingness to pay.

## Evidence Used

The publisher abstract reports matched vintage-postcard lots sold by the same
operator under different eBay identities. The high-reputation identity earned
an 8.1 percent higher willingness-to-pay outcome.

## Relevance To Operator Node Hypothesis

This weakly supports the narrow mechanism that platform reputation systems can
reduce some trust and asymmetric-information frictions in market exchange. It
does not show AI-specific transaction-cost reduction or firm-boundary change.

## Objections Or Limitations

- The setting is eBay vintage-postcard auctions, not professional services,
  enterprise procurement, or Operator Node contracting.
- The measured outcome is price/willingness to pay, not dispute rate,
  enforcement cost, rework, liability, or procurement cycle time.
- The source supports reputation mechanisms, not audit trails, escrow,
  insurance, SLAs, certification, or AI verification.
- Full text remains pending, so detailed design and limitation claims should
  not exceed the publisher abstract.
