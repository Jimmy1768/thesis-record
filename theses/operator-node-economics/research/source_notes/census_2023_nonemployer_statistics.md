## Core Claims

- Nonemployer Statistics is an annual Census data product for U.S. businesses
  with no paid employees.
- NES reports number of establishments and total receipts by geography and
  industry.
- The Census describes most nonemployers as self-employed individuals operating
  unincorporated sole proprietorships, though partnerships and corporations
  can also be nonemployers.
- NES coverage requires no paid employees, federal income-tax liability, and
  receipts of at least $1,000, except Construction, where the threshold is $1.
- Beginning with reference year 2023, AIES-NES provides national sales,
  shipments, or revenue data for employer and nonemployer businesses in
  comparable industries.

## Evidence Used

The program page states that NES provides annual subnational economic data for
U.S. businesses with no paid employees by industry and includes establishments
and total receipts.

The about page states that NES is the only annual detailed and comprehensive
source on U.S. businesses with no paid employees subject to federal income tax.
It also states that most nonemployers are self-employed individuals operating
unincorporated businesses and that data include establishment counts and total
receipts.

The API documentation confirms 2023 availability and that data include
nonemployer establishments and receipts by 2022 NAICS, geography, legal form
of organization, and receipt-size class. It warns that the 2022 release
incorporated methodological improvements affecting historical comparability.

## Relevance To Operator Node Hypothesis

NES is the key official U.S. baseline for nonemployer receipts and counts. It
can help test whether no-paid-employee businesses gain share or receipts in
AI-exposed sectors, but it does not identify AI use, one-human automation
stacks, hidden contractors, quality, survival, or firm-boundary transitions.

## Objections Or Limitations

- NES does not measure AI adoption or AI-enabled workflows.
- "No paid employees" is not identical to one-human Operator Node status.
- Receipts do not reveal profits, labor hours, contractors, quality, or
  transaction costs.
- The source does not by itself measure survival, churn, or transition from
  nonemployer to employer firm.
- The 2022 methodology change limits naive long-run trend comparisons.
