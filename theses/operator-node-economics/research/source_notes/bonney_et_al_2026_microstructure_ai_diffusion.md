## Bibliographic Reference

Kathryn Bonney, Cory Breaux, Emin Dinlersoz, Lucia Foster, John Haltiwanger,
and Aditya Pande, "The Microstructure of AI Diffusion: Evidence from Firms,
Business Functions, and Worker Tasks," U.S. Census Bureau Center for Economic
Studies Working Paper CES-26-25, April 2026.

## Source Type

Official agency working paper using nationally representative BTOS AI
supplement data.

## Evidence Category

- Task productivity: no direct productivity measurement; task use categories
  include augmentation, substitution, creation, writing, document analysis, and
  information search.
- Transaction-cost evidence: no direct market-transaction measurement.
- Firm-boundary evidence: partial employer-firm labor and capital outcome
  evidence; no nonemployer, outsourcing, management-layer, or node-to-firm
  transition evidence.
- Adoption-by-size evidence: yes.

## Core Claims

- AI adoption is materially higher in large firms and knowledge-intensive
  sectors.
- AI use often remains narrow across business functions and worker tasks.
- Most AI-using firms report augmentation rather than substitution.
- Reported AI-related employment decreases are rare.
- Broader functional integration and operational investment correlate with
  commercial performance and with employment-decrease outcomes, but the paper
  is explicit that the regressions are associational rather than causal.

## Evidence Used

The paper uses the second AI supplement to the Census Bureau's BTOS, with a
November 2025 to January 2026 supplement reference period. It reports that 18
percent of firms used AI in a business function, rising to 32 percent on an
employment-weighted basis. It reports substantially higher AI use in large
firms and in Information, Professional Services, and Finance.

The paper reports that among AI-using firms, 66 percent rely solely on
augmentation for task effects. AI-related employment decreases occur in about
2 percent of firms. Among AI-using firms, any headcount impact is reported by
about 5 percent, split roughly between increases and decreases. The paper also
reports that broader functional use and operational investment have positive
associations with employment decreases, while worker-task breadth has no
significant association with headcount reduction after controls.

## Relevance To Operator Node Hypothesis

This is stronger evidence for a corporate-absorption and large-firm adoption
criticism than the earlier Census story alone. It also weakens any claim that
current AI adoption is already producing broad firm-boundary shrinkage or
large-scale employer headcount displacement.

## Objections Or Limitations

- CES working papers have not undergone the review accorded Census Bureau
  publications, though disclosure review was completed.
- The regressions are conditional associations, not causal estimates.
- The data cover employer firms, not nonemployer one-human Operator Nodes.
- Employment effects are self-reported by firms and do not measure management
  layers, outsourcing, vendor substitution, or firm-boundary transitions.
- Worker-use measures are reported by firm respondents, not independently by
  employees.
