## Evidence Category

- Task productivity: yes.
- Transaction-cost evidence: limited internal proxy only; customer requests to
  speak to a manager fall under support-process escalation, not market
  contracting or enforcement.
- Firm-boundary evidence: no.
- Adoption-by-size evidence: no primary evidence from this study; the article
  cites other adoption literature but does not itself measure economy-wide firm
  adoption.

## Core Claims

- Generative AI increased productivity in a customer-support setting.
- Reported effects were heterogeneous and occurred inside a firm setting.
- The study is stronger evidence for task execution and knowledge-transfer
  effects than for firm-boundary shifts.

## Evidence Used

The directly inspected QJE article studies staggered deployment of a
GPT-3-based assistant for 5,172 customer-support agents in a Fortune 500 firm
and its subcontracting context. It reports a 15 percent increase in resolved
issues per hour on average, larger gains for less experienced and lower-skilled
agents, some small quality decline for the highest-skill agents, faster
learning, more polite customer interactions, fewer requests to speak to a
supervisor, and lower attrition for newer workers.

The article explicitly limits external validity: it captures medium-run effects
in one firm and does not observe wages, overall labor demand, or changes in the
skill composition of hiring.

## Relevance To Operator Node Hypothesis

This supports the narrow claim that AI can reduce some task, execution,
training, and knowledge-transfer costs inside an organization. It does not
directly support the stronger claim that one-human nodes can outcompete firms,
lower market transaction costs, or shift firm boundaries.

## Objections Or Limitations

- The study occurs inside a Fortune 500 firm.
- Firm-provided workflows, data, monitoring, and institutional context may be
  part of the treatment environment.
- It does not measure external contracting, enforcement, liability, or
  procurement costs.
- It does not measure firm size, nonemployer entry, outsourcing share,
  management-layer change, market concentration, or independent-node survival.
