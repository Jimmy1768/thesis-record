## Source Type

Primary transaction-cost economics article.

## Core Claims

- Governance should be analyzed comparatively across markets, hybrids, and
  hierarchies as discrete structural alternatives.
- Governance forms differ by incentive intensity, administrative control,
  adaptation attributes, and contract-law regime.
- Asset specificity is a central transaction dimension because it creates
  bilateral dependency and contracting hazards.
- Markets are comparatively strong when asset specificity is low; hierarchy
  gains as coordinated adaptation becomes more important; hybrids occupy an
  intermediate range.
- Hybrids preserve some autonomy and market-like incentives, but rely on
  safeguards and are vulnerable when disturbances require repeated mutual
  consent.
- Reputation can improve nonhierarchical or hybrid contracting, but Williamson
  warns that reputation effects are easy to overstate.
- Institutional-environment changes in property rights, contract law,
  reputation, and uncertainty can shift comparative governance costs.

## Safe For Future Prose

- Williamson 1991 can now support market, hybrid, and hierarchy as generic
  governance forms in comparative governance analysis.
- It can support the claim that governance forms differ by incentives,
  administrative controls, adaptation attributes, and contract-law regime.
- It can support asset specificity as a transaction dimension that creates
  bilateral dependency and shifts the efficient governance form from market to
  hybrid to hierarchy as specificity rises.
- It can support hybrid governance as intermediate and semi-legalistic: hybrids
  preserve some autonomy and incentives while adding safeguards and
  administrative apparatus.
- It can support the claim that hierarchy has advantages for coordinated
  adaptation under bilateral dependency, but at the cost of weaker incentives
  and added bureaucracy.
- It can support the claim that reputation effects may improve hybrid or
  nonhierarchical contracting but should not be overstated.
- It can support the claim that frequent or high-variance disturbances can make
  hybrid governance vulnerable because mutual-consent adaptation takes time.
- It can support institutional-environment shifts as parameters that alter
  comparative governance costs.

## Claims Needing Caution

- Williamson 1991 does not directly support AI adoption, AI productivity,
  Operator Nodes, protocol networks, audit trails, or digital automation.
- Claims about Operator Nodes should use this source only for governance
  mechanisms and boundary conditions, not as evidence that AI shifts firm
  boundaries.
- Williamson's hybrid model does not erase Powell 1990's warning that network
  forms can be distinct from both markets and hierarchies. If Operator Node
  networks are discussed, classification should distinguish TCE hybrids,
  Powell-style network forms, platforms, firms, and ordinary vendor markets.
- The asset-specificity result is comparative, not deterministic; hierarchy
  adds bureaucratic costs and weaker incentives.
- Macher/Richman 2008 remains full-text blocked and should not be upgraded from
  this Williamson pass.
