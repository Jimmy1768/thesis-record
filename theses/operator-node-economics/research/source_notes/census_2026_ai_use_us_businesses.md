## Evidence Category

- Task productivity: no.
- Transaction-cost evidence: no.
- Firm-boundary evidence: weak indirect adoption-by-size evidence only; no

  concentration outcome.
- Adoption-by-size evidence: yes, for U.S. employer businesses covered by BTOS.

## Core Claims

- Recent Business Trends and Outlook Survey data report AI use across U.S.
  businesses.
- The article reports higher AI use among larger employer firms than among the
  smallest employer firms.
- The November 2025 wording change means trend comparisons require caution.

## Evidence Used

The directly inspected Census article reports BTOS data collected from
December 14, 2025 to May 3, 2026. Overall reported AI use hovered between 17
and 20 percent, while expected six-month use ranged from 20 to 23 percent. In
the period ending May 3, 2026, 37 percent of firms with at least 250 employees
reported AI use and 32 percent of firms with 100 to 249 employees reported AI
use; fewer than 20 percent of firms with four or fewer employees reported AI
use.

The article states that, beginning November 17, 2025, the BTOS revised its AI
wording from use in producing goods or services to use in any business
function. The AI supplement asks about 15 business functions and operational
changes such as staff training, workflow changes, technology investments, and
vendor or consulting services for AI integration.

## Relevance To Operator Node Hypothesis

This source weakly supports the criticism that larger employer firms may adopt
AI faster than very small employer firms. It does not show productivity capture,
profit capture, nonemployer performance, or firm-boundary change.

## Objections Or Limitations

- Adoption does not prove productivity, profit capture, or firm-boundary
  effects.
- Survey wording changes complicate trend comparisons.
- Firm size, age, industry, and respondent interpretation may confound results.
- BTOS covers employer businesses; it does not directly measure one-human
  nonemployer Operator Nodes.
