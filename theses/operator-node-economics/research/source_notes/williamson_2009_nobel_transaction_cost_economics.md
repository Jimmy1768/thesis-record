## Core Claims

- Transaction-cost economics uses the lens of contract to compare governance
  arrangements.
- Bounded rationality, opportunism, adaptation, and governance alignment remain
  central.
- Outsourcing can fail when transactions are highly specialized, incomplete,
  interdependent, or exposed to consequential disturbances.
- TCE should generate predictions and empirical tests rather than ex post
  explanations.

## Direct Reinspection

Directly reinspected on 2026-06-11 from the official Nobel Prize lecture PDF.

Pages/sections inspected:

- pp. 455-457: overview, governance, lens of contract, transaction as unit of

  organization.
- pp. 460-462: rudiments of TCE, incomplete contracts, bounded rationality,

- pp. 463-466: asset specificity, bilateral dependency, market and hierarchy
  syndromes, and governance alignment.
- pp. 470-471: forbearance, hierarchy as internal dispute forum, and Boeing 787
  outsourcing caveat.

Safe for future prose:

- Williamson frames TCE as comparative contractual governance.
- The transaction is the unit of analysis, with markets and hierarchies as
  alternative governance modes.
- Complex contracts are incomplete because of bounded rationality.
- Asset specificity and outlier disturbances make some incompleteness
  consequential.
- Specific assets create bilateral dependency and can compromise simple market
  exchange.
- Outsourcing can fail when highly specialized components require adaptation.

Claims still needing caution:

- The 2009/2010 lecture supports markets and hierarchies strongly, but it does
  not provide the full 1991 market/hybrid/hierarchy table.
- It does not directly support claims about AI, Operator Nodes, or protocol
  networks.
- The Boeing example is manufacturing/system-integration evidence, not direct
  evidence about digital Operator Nodes.

Interpretations weakened or corrected:

- TCE should be presented as comparative governance and prediction/testing, not
  as a slogan that markets beat firms.
- Asset specificity is a stronger boundary condition than the earlier broad
  "transaction costs collapse" framing.

## Relevance To Operator Node Hypothesis

The lecture strengthens the requirement that Operator Node claims be framed as
testable governance predictions. It also supplies a strong criticism: modular
outsourcing can fail when system-level coordination and specialized adaptation
matter.

## Objections Or Limitations

- Retrospective lectures synthesize a mature framework but are not direct tests
  of AI-enabled organizations.
- The Boeing 787 outsourcing caveat may generalize imperfectly to digital
  services, so applicability depends on transaction interdependence.
