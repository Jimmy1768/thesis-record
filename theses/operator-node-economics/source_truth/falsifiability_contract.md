# Falsifiability Contract

The theory must generate measurable predictions.

A prediction is not acceptable if it cannot be tested, measured, or contradicted.

## Versioned Forecast Rule

Operator Node Economics should be treated as a versioned forecast research
program.

- V1: publish the thesis, mechanisms, predictions, evidence requirements, and
  failure conditions before the outcome is known.
- V2: publish the first evidence check whether results are favorable, mixed,
  null, or adverse.
- V3: publish revision, narrowing, or rejection based on the accumulated
  evidence.

Negative updates are valid research outputs. The program should not hide failed
predictions or preserve a thesis after its failure conditions are triggered.
Prestige, awards, and historical importance are not evidence and must not
delay, soften, or obscure falsification.

The user's company or network is one possible implementation case. It may
provide feasibility evidence, but success or failure of that single case is not
identical to success or failure of the broad thesis.

## Candidate Prediction Areas

- Employee count per unit output declines.
- Small operator nodes outperform traditional firms in specific industries.
- Capital requirements fall for some productive enterprises.
- Protocol-based coordination gains market share.
- Corporate hierarchy shrinks in targeted sectors.
- Remote employment in AI-exposed, output-measurable digital work declines as
  firms substitute node, vendor, owner-operator, or AI-labor-service capacity.
- Economic roles shift toward creators, niche human providers, and experiencers
  if automated production makes human preference validation more scarce than
  production capacity.

## Coasean Relative-Cost Test

The strong Operator Node hypothesis requires more than evidence that AI makes
individuals faster. It requires evidence that AI reduces market/protocol
transaction costs more than it reduces internal corporate organization costs.

Task-productivity evidence cannot be treated as firm-boundary evidence unless
transaction-cost metrics are also measured.

## Required Prediction Fields

Every prediction should eventually specify:

- claim;
- unit of measurement;
- expected direction of change;
- comparison group;
- timeframe;
- data source;
- failure condition;
- confounders.

## Failure Conditions

The theory weakens if evidence shows:

- AI lowers task costs but does not reduce coordination or hierarchy costs;
- corporations absorb AI advantages better than independent nodes;
- legal, capital, trust, liability, or market-access constraints dominate;
- protocol-based networks fail to maintain quality or accountability;
- operator nodes cannot scale beyond narrow services;
- transaction costs move rather than collapse.
- large firms capture most AI productivity gains;
- high-performing nodes convert into firms;
- remote salaried employment remains durable or grows in AI-exposed,
  output-measurable digital roles after capable AI labor services become
  available;
- no meaningful market for compensated human preference validation,
  experiential testing, or niche human provision emerges despite abundant
  AI-produced output;
- broad human evaluation is replaced mainly by telemetry, synthetic users, or
  elite tastemakers rather than becoming a distributed labor category;
- AI-enabled nodes show no reduction in contracting, monitoring, dispute,
  rework, trust, or enforcement costs relative to firm vendors;
- protocol networks show high dispute, rework, or enforcement costs;
- high-asset-specificity work remains integrated inside firms or long-term
  hierarchical relationships.
