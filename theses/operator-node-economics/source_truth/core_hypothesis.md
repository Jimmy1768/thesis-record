# Core Hypothesis

## Working Hypothesis

The corporation may not be the final or optimal organizational form for
economic production.

Historically, major economic systems emerged when new technologies changed the
cost of coordination. Examples include:

- tribe and kinship groups;
- city-state structures;
- feudal estates;
- mercantile states;
- industrial corporations.

Each system solved coordination problems under the technological constraints of
its era.

The working hypothesis is:

> AI dramatically reduces the cost of reasoning, execution, documentation,
> coordination, memory, and expertise. As these costs fall, the corporation may
> lose some of its historical advantage. A new coordination unit may emerge.

## Stronger Network Thesis

The stronger claim is not that one operator becomes more productive.

The stronger claim is:

> Multiple operator nodes may coordinate through protocols rather than
> hierarchy.

Historically, large-scale production often required employees, managers,
directors, executives, and corporate hierarchy.

The proposed future may rely more on independent operator nodes cooperating
through shared standards, shared protocols, shared software, shared memory
systems, and AI-mediated coordination.

## Remote Employment As Transitional Form

The strong thesis treats remote employment as an intermediate unbundling, not
as a competing explanation.

Office employment bundles work, employment, hierarchy, and physical
co-location. Remote employment removes physical co-location but usually keeps
the worker inside the firm through salary, HR, payroll, management, internal
permissions, and corporate authority.

The stronger Operator Node forecast is that some AI-exposed, digitally
deliverable, output-measurable remote employee roles should later be unbundled
from employment itself. Firms would buy output, capacity, node services, or AI
labor services rather than hire remote salaried employees for those roles.

Therefore, durable growth or persistence of remote employment in sectors where
AI labor systems can perform the work is a falsifier for the strong thesis,
not merely a confounder.

## Economic Class Implications

If the stronger thesis is true, the relevant economic roles may shift away from
a simple owner/laborer distinction toward a forecast taxonomy of roles:

- creators: people or entities that initiate, own, design, orchestrate, or
  compound productive systems;
- niche human providers: humans paid because humanness, rarity, performance,
  care, embodied presence, trust, ritual, or prestige is part of the product;
- experiencers: humans paid to evaluate, validate, compare, review, certify,
  or communicate whether AI-produced or node-produced outputs are desirable to
  humans.

This taxonomy is conditional and speculative. It is not a political theory,
policy recommendation, moral hierarchy, or conclusion. It belongs in the
versioned forecast program only if it is expressed with measurable predictions
and failure conditions.

The core mechanism is: when automated systems can produce more outputs at lower
cost, the scarce human input may shift from making the thing to deciding
whether the thing is desirable, trusted, meaningful, comfortable, beautiful,
safe, prestigious, or worth choosing.

Policy responses such as universal basic income, productivity taxes, or
employment-penalty taxes are out of scope for the core thesis unless a separate
policy paper is authorized.

## Current Status

Unproven. Treat as a versioned forecast hypothesis to test, not a conclusion.

V1 should publish the forecast baseline, mechanisms, prediction horizons,
evidence requirements, and failure conditions before the answer is known.
Future versions should report whether the thesis is supported, narrowed,
contradicted, or rejected.

The user's company is one implementation case, not the whole thesis. Its
success or failure may inform the research program, but any credible successful
case moving in the predicted direction can matter for the broader thesis.
